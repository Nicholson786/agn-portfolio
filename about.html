I came up through the floor.

Not through a leadership development program. Not as an analyst looking down at a process from a dashboard. I started in operations and manufacturing and learned what systems look like when they run — and what they look like when they're held together by workarounds nobody remembers creating.

Seventeen years later, I engineer the process itself.

I hold degrees in Industrial Engineering Technology from the University of Southern Mississippi and Engineering Management from the University of Oklahoma — not as credentials to display, but as frameworks that sharpened how I see operational systems. I'm completing a Doctorate in Business Administration at Edgewood University, with research focused on continuous improvement, because the field deserves rigorous academic inquiry and the rigor makes the operational work better.

I hold a Lean Six Sigma Black Belt and am preparing to sit for my PMP — not because I need another certification, but because the standard matters. I am an active member of both the Institute of Industrial and Systems Engineers and the Project Management Institute, and I attend the rooms where the discipline is being shaped. AI integration in operational and engineering practice was a central focus at the IISE Annual Conference in Dallas this year. I was there. I'm paying attention.

When existing tools weren't enough, I built new ones. SQL for the data. Raspberry Pi for the prototypes. Three operational applications — one live, two in development — that directly address gaps no off-the-shelf solution was solving.

I've also written about it. Twice. Beyond the Bottleneck and Legacy by Improvement aren't academic exercises — they're distilled field knowledge published for practitioners who are doing the work and need frameworks that hold up under real operational pressure.

My cumulative purpose — formalized in a Professional Code of Ethics through my doctoral program — is to lead with integrity, elevate others, and ensure that my influence contributes to a culture of trust, accountability, and sustainable performance.

This is what seventeen years of deliberate, compounding improvement looks like.
